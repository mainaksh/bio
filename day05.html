The performance baseline. Gradient-boosted trees with histogram method; usually 2-4 AUROC points above RF on these tabular cheminformatics features. Trains in <30s on the largest endpoint (hERG); calibration via isotonic on val split.

tree_method: hist n_estimators: 1000 (early-stop @ 50) max_depth: 6 learning_rate: 0.05 scale_pos_weight: auto (balanced) eval_metric: aucpr random_state: 42

Two algorithms is the minimum credible baseline for binary toxicity prediction. RF gives interpretability (feature importances, partial dependence) and is hard to beat with default hyperparameters; XGBoost adds the standard performance ceiling over RF on tabular features. Anything more than two algorithms today is over-engineering for v0 — the decision rule is "best-of-two registered active." Day 6's GAT and ChemBERTa join the comparison Week 2; Day 17's calibration ensembles join Week 4.

The split table is the truth that matters. Scaffold-split by Bemis–Murcko (Day-3 curation protocol) gives ~70/15/15 with no scaffold leakage between train and test, which is what makes these AUROC numbers worth anything. Random splits on the same data would inflate every cell by 4-8 AUROC points and produce a graph that looks better and would mislead anyone reading it — including ourselves on Day 17 calibration day.

Note the imbalance pills. Five endpoints sit below 5% positive rate (NR-AR 3.0%, NR-AR-LBD 3.5%, NR-PPAR-gamma 3.0%, NR-Aromatase 3.7%, SR-ATAD5 3.5%); on these, AUPRC matters more than AUROC and we expect XGBoost's scale_pos_weight + histogram method to dominate RF by a clear margin. One endpoint (FDA_APPROVED) is 93% positive class — it is the easiest classifier to write and the least informative. We train it for completeness; it never gates a decision.

Class imbalance on rare endpoints — AUROC alone misleads.

Predicted 5 endpoints sit at < 5% positive rate. A model predicting all-negative would get high accuracy, near-random AUROC, and produce nothing useful. The risk: report optimistic AUROC numbers that don't translate to decision-support utility. Mitigation: gate every endpoint on AUPRC ≥ 1.5× positive-rate-baseline.
What happened All 16 endpoints clear the gate. Smallest AUPRC lift = NR-PPAR-gamma at 9.6× baseline (AUPRC 0.288 vs 0.030 positive rate). XGBoost's scale_pos_weight + histogram method dominated RF on every rare-class endpoint as predicted. Brier scores under 0.05 on the rarest classes confirm calibration is honest, not opportunistic.
Disposition Retired — AUPRC gate clears with margin on every endpoint. No endpoint flagged for "needs Day-6 deep model" on AUPRC grounds; Day 6 GAT + ChemBERTa contend on AUROC delta only.
DR-5.2 retired

Featurization-time mismatch between training and inference.

Predicted Sarthak's training runs on vm-worker-pool with one RDKit version; /predict on vm-core-api would compute features with possibly a different version. Even single-bit feature-vector differences produce nonsense predictions. Mitigation: tox-inference owns featurization; core-api proxies; both inherit from aitip-base:0.1.0 so RDKit version is shared.
What happened Implemented as planned. tox-inference/aitip/tox/api.py imports featurize_one directly from services.data.featurize — the same function the Day-3 featurize.py used, the same function the Day-4 Celery task used. Aspirin's hERG prediction at 16:42 IST shows feature-vector-on-the-wire contract is intact end-to-end.
Disposition Retired — single-source-of-truth for featurization across all paths. Pattern carries forward: any future model (GAT, ChemBERTa, ensembles) puts its feature computation in tox-inference, not in core-api.
DR-5.3 retired

MLflow's Postgres backend conflicts with migration 001 schema.

Predicted MLflow 2.x creates ~14 tables in its target db; if it picks up public schema, table names like experiments or runs could conflict with future migration tables. Mitigation: explicit --backend-store-uri postgresql://...?options=-c%20search_path%3Dmlflow isolating MLflow to its own schema.
