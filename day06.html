is_active=TRUE ↔ argmax(aurocarch) subject to aurocwinner − aurocxgb ≥ 0.02

The 0.02 threshold isn't arbitrary — it's roughly the standard deviation of XGBoost AUROC across random seeds on these datasets, so anything inside that band could plausibly be measurement noise. Replacing a fast 30-second-train baseline with a slow 18-minute-train transformer demands that the AUROC improvement be detectable above noise. If no architecture clears the gate on an endpoint, yesterday's XGBoost retains is_active=TRUE — the count of active models stays exactly 16 throughout the day, regardless of how today's training goes.

The composability claim from yesterday's foundation-close prose is what makes today possible at all. Yesterday's XGBoost active models don't go away; they sit in model_registry alongside today's GAT and ChemBERTa contenders, with is_active=FALSE if displaced. Day 17 calibration day re-visits the field for ensembling; Day 10 mid-POC review walks the historical record. Nothing about today's outcome breaks anything we shipped Day 5.

Three architectures, three different ways to turn a SMILES into a probability. GAT learns directly from molecular graph structure; ChemBERTa imports prior knowledge from millions of unlabeled ZINC compounds and adapts it to toxicity; XGBoost reads the same hand-crafted molecular features chemists have used for thirty years. By 17:30 the platform either has new champions per endpoint or confirms that XGBoost on Morgan+RDKit features is the right v0 choice. Both outcomes are useful information; neither is failure.

Worth noting what is not in this diagram. No GPU acceleration (V1 Horizon-01); both deep architectures train on CPU today, accepting the ~10x slowdown vs published GPU benchmarks. No ensembling (Day 17 work). No multi-task heads (the canonical SMILES-to-probability binary task is what every architecture solves; cross-endpoint transfer learning is V2). No model distillation (V1 latency engineering). Today is the simple comparison: same input, same gate, three architectures.

§ 06 · day-6 risk disposition

Three risks named — two retired, one mitigated

Three engineers, three risks. Two retired in-day by execution discipline; one mitigated structurally with explicit Day-11 carry. Zero fires.

DR-6.1 retired

ChemBERTa fine-tune may not converge in 3 epochs on small endpoints.

Predicted ClinTox 1,032 train rows, DILIrank 723 train rows; 3 epochs from a chemistry-pretrained-but-not-toxicity-pretrained checkpoint may be too few for the classification head to reach a useful state. Risk: epoch-1 returns near-random AUROC and the gate misses by a wide margin. Mitigation: scale epoch budget by inverse dataset size (6 for small, 3 for large); abort + re-tune if epoch-1 val-AUROC < 0.55.
What happened Mitigation worked exactly as designed. Every endpoint's epoch-1 val-AUROC came in well above 0.55 (the lowest was DILIrank at 0.61, 6-epoch budget); zero early-aborts. ClinTox CT_TOX final AUROC 0.751 (vs target 0.72); FDA_APPROVED 0.738 (vs 0.70); DILIrank dili 0.708 (vs 0.65). All three small-endpoint targets met despite the small training corpus.
Disposition Retired — epoch-budget scaling pattern committed in services/models/chemberta.py's _epoch_budget(). Same pattern carries forward to any future small-corpus fine-tune (Day-9 LLM SFT considered); threshold revisited at Day-17 calibration day with proper data.
DR-6.2 mitigated

GAT model checkpoints 50-200 MB each — 16x bigger than XGBoost joblibs.

Predicted tox-inference v0.2.0 hard-coded functools.lru_cache(maxsize=32) sized for few-MB joblibs; 16 GAT checkpoints alone push memory closer to 3 GB resident. Cold-start on a fresh container becomes user-visible (10+ second model-load instead of millisecond cache hit). Mitigation: shrink lru_cache to maxsize=8 for deep-model entries today; longer-term Day-11 work adds startup pre-warm hook.
What happened Today's Router (PR-#21) ships with maxsize=8 on all three loader caches independently. With ~24 hot models maximum across all three architectures (8 xgboost + 8 gat + 8 chemberta), worst-case resident is ~2 GB on vm-tox-inference's 128 GB partition — trivially fits. Cold-start latency mitigation deferred to Day-11 startup pre-warm hook (carry-forward on the Day-11 entry in mid-POC review prep).
Disposition Mitigated — memory-pressure ceiling addressed structurally; cold-start latency carries to Day 11. Pattern is "one issue, two phases": today protects against memory blowup, Day-11 attacks the user-visible cold-start.
DR-6.3 retired

vm-tox-inference NUMA pin to node 0 may not survive 32-task burst.

Predicted ChemBERTa fine-tune at 4-thread torch on each of 8 worker pods = 32 simultaneous threads; vm-tox-inference has 16 cores on NUMA node 0 (Day-2 ADR pin). Kernel scheduler may push processes onto node-1 cores, hurting cache locality and slowing training 30-50%. Mitigation: numastat sampling during burst; fall back to OMP_NUM_THREADS=2 per worker if leakage detected.
What happened Numastat sampling at 60-second intervals through the entire 13:42-14:58 burst showed node-1 memory stayed under 3 MB throughout (essentially shared library pages, not active workload). All training task processes consumed memory exclusively on node 0. The cgroup NUMA pin from Day-2 ADR holds under realistic deep-model workload; OMP_NUM_THREADS=2 fallback never invoked.
Disposition Retired — NUMA pin verified empirically under realistic load. Same pattern applies to any future high-thread-count workload on vm-tox-inference (Day-7 batch embedding, Day-11 orchestrator dispatch, V1 GPU node migration when it lands).

Pattern noted: model-shaped risks vs infrastructure-shaped risks. The Foundation phase (Days 1-5) named 17 risks of which 14 were infrastructure-shaped (cooling, NUMA, schema isolation, etc); today's 3 are split between model-shaped (DR-6.1 ChemBERTa convergence) and infrastructure-shaped (DR-6.2 memory pressure, DR-6.3 NUMA leakage). The standup framing held: today's bridge plans were model-shaped (delta gate is itself the safety mechanism; failure means we learn XGBoost is the right v0 choice for some endpoints — not a foundation-level failure).
