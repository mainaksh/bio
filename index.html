§ 00 · framing

What we are building, and why now

A short, honest brief before the day-by-day plan. Read this once; the rest of the index assumes it.

The pharmaceutical industry loses roughly thirty percent of its drug-development pipeline to toxicity that surfaces only after years of investment. ToxCast and Tox21 have made the underlying assay data public; ChemBERTa, MolFormer, and graph neural networks have made predictive modeling state-of-the-art; and on-prem LLMs have, in the last eighteen months, become genuinely usable on commodity CPU hardware. The platform we are building stitches those three trends into a single tool a medicinal chemist or a regulatory toxicologist can actually drive.

This is not a demo and it is not a research notebook. By Day 20 the system runs as a set of containerized services on a single host, serves typed REST endpoints behind authentication, persists every prediction with full audit metadata, and renders a working web dashboard backed by the same APIs an external integrator would consume. The architecture is deliberately partitioned so that adding a GPU node and migrating the language-model service is a one-day operation, not a rebuild.

We are shipping the same system, on the same host, that V1 will scale — not a throwaway proof we will throw away.

Three things keep this scope honest in twenty days. One: we lean on open public datasets (Tox21, ToxCast, ClinTox, SIDER, hERG/ChEMBL, DILIrank) rather than scraping or licensing private corpora. Two: we use frozen pre-trained encoders (ChemBERTa-2, MolFormer-XL, PubMedBERT) for similarity and retrieval rather than training from scratch — we fine-tune only the classification heads. Three: the local LLM (Llama-3.1-8B-Instruct, 4-bit quantized via vLLM) is configured to refuse answers without retrieved citations, which removes an entire category of demo-day risk.

The deliverable on Day 20 is a forty-five-minute live demonstration that exercises every one of the seven capabilities end-to-end on real, named compounds (Doxorubicin, Aspirin, Thalidomide, Cisplatin) and returns calibrated, cited, explainable answers in under fifteen seconds per molecule. The handover is the running stack plus operator runbooks, model cards, audit log exports, and a roadmap to V1 (GPU, generative design, multi-tenant SaaS, regulatory-pack export).

§ 03 · hardware partitioning

How the 72-core host is carved up

One purpose-built VM per concern, all on a private bridge, all snapshot-able. The reserve row leaves room for a future GPU node, scale-out, and snapshots without rebuilding.

The host is a single physical machine: 72 CPU cores (assume two NUMA nodes of 36 cores each, hyperthreading on, so 144 logical CPUs — we allocate by physical cores and pin); 1 TB of RAM; 10 TB of NVMe SSD. Day 1 confirms the actual NUMA layout with numactl --hardware, the disk topology with lsblk, and the firmware-level virtualization extensions (VT-x or AMD-V, IOMMU on for future GPU passthrough). Day 2 carves it. After Day 2 we never touch the host's core configuration again — everything else lives inside a VM with a documented resource cap.

We use KVM with libvirt (Proxmox VE is acceptable as an alternative if Jitendra prefers its console). VM disks live on a ZFS thin-pool so snapshots are nearly free, point-in-time, and roll back in seconds. The internal bridge br-poc0 on 10.20.0.0/24 carries inter-VM traffic; only the reverse-proxy VM exposes ports to the host network. nftables on every VM defaults to deny-inbound, with explicit allows for the ports a VM's role actually requires.

The eight-VM partition below totals exactly 72 vCPU, leaves about 4 GB of RAM for the hypervisor itself, and consumes 9 TB of the 10 TB SSD with the remaining 1 TB held in the ZFS pool's free margin so snapshots do not fail under load. A reserve allocation ((reserve)) holds 220 GB of RAM and 4.2 TB of disk — this is intentional headroom for adding the GPU passthrough VM in V1, for a second worker pool if batch demand spikes during the demo, and for the snapshot-and-rollback discipline during model training and dataset ingestion.

NUMA pinning. The two heaviest VMs — vm-tox-inference (16 vCPU) and vm-rag-llm (12 vCPU) — are pinned to opposite NUMA nodes via cpuset and numatune in their libvirt domain XML. This prevents cross-socket memory traffic during the heaviest single-process workloads (PyTorch tensor operations on the inference VM, vLLM kv-cache reads on the LLM VM). The remaining VMs are scheduled normally; the kernel's NUMA balancing handles them.

Snapshot discipline. Every VM is snapshotted with the suffix -base immediately after Jitendra's golden-image bootstrap completes. Before any irreversible operation — a kernel update, a Postgres schema migration, an experimental model training run that uses a new RDKit version — the affected VM's owner takes a fresh snapshot first. The ZFS pool is sized so all 8 VMs can each hold three full snapshots without exhausting free space. Rollback is a one-line operation tested on Day 3.

Network plan. All 8 VMs sit on br-poc0 with statically allocated IPs in 10.20.0.0/24 (recorded in dnsmasq host entries so vm-data-store.poc.local resolves correctly from any other VM). Only vm-bastion exposes port 22 to the host network for SSH. Only vm-core-api via Traefik exposes 80/443 on the host network. Every other VM is reachable only from inside the bridge. Worker VM egress (NCBI E-utilities, HuggingFace model downloads) is allowed via a NAT rule on the host but logged.

Why FastAPI over Flask or Django REST. FastAPI is async-first (free with our Python 3.12 baseline), generates OpenAPI 3.1 with no extra effort, and Pydantic v2 catches schema bugs at request time rather than at integration time. Harshit shipped this exact framework at Zivoke.

Why ChromaDB over Qdrant or Weaviate or pgvector. The corpus is 100K chunks — well within ChromaDB's sweet spot — and ChromaDB has no operational footprint to speak of. Qdrant wins at million-chunk scale with complex filtering, but we are not there. pgvector would have been fine but adds load to the same Postgres instance that holds our transactional state, which we do not want to mix. Weaviate is overbuilt for our needs.

Why HNSWlib over FAISS for molecular similarity. HNSWlib has lower RAM overhead at our index size (1M × 128-dim ≈ 0.5 GB), simpler persistence to disk, and reliable recall@10 ≥ 0.98 with the M=32, efC=200 configuration we have validated in prior work. FAISS is faster on GPU but we do not have a GPU yet; on CPU at our scale, HNSWlib wins on simplicity-to-recall ratio.

Why Llama-3.1-8B-Instruct over a hosted model. Customer-data privacy is a hard constraint: pharmaceutical chemistry is patent-sensitive, and we cannot route SMILES strings through a third-party API. The 4-bit AWQ quantization fits in 6 GB of RAM and runs on CPU at acceptable latency for the demo. When the GPU node lands in V1, the same model weights move — no swap, no re-evaluation.

Why Streamlit before React. A working Streamlit dashboard on Day 12 is worth two weeks of "almost-finished" React. We get stakeholder feedback five days earlier, and the React build (Days 13–18) ports proven flows rather than chasing a moving design. Streamlit is archived on Day 18.

§ 05 · datasets

Where the signal comes from

Public, well-curated, regulator-friendly datasets. Every record is canonicalized, scaffold-split, and versioned in MinIO. No private licenses, no scraped data, no pay-walled corpora — the whole pipeline is reproducible by anyone with our codebase and an internet connection.

Toxicity ML lives or dies by data discipline. The same compound appears in five public datasets under three SMILES tautomers and two assay protocols; if we naively union them, the model memorizes the duplicate and reports an inflated AUROC that collapses on the next batch. Day 4 is dedicated entirely to curation: SMILES canonicalization (RDKit MolToSmiles with canonical=True, salt stripped via SaltRemover, charge-neutralized via Uncharger), duplicate merge with a documented conflict-resolution rule (when labels disagree, drop the compound and log the rejection), and Bemis–Murcko scaffold splits for the 70/15/15 train/val/test partition.

We use scaffold splits, not random splits, because pharmaceutical reality is that the next compound a chemist asks us about will not look like anything in our training set. A random 70/15/15 split inflates AUROC by 5–10 points compared to scaffold-holdout on the same data; scaffold-holdout is what regulators and serious medicinal chemists trust. Our published metrics will all be scaffold-holdout, with random-split numbers reported separately for benchmarking against the public literature.

Tox21
~ 8,000 compounds · 12 endpoints (NR + SR)

Multi-task in vitro toxicity classification. Seven nuclear-receptor (androgen, estrogen, glucocorticoid, aromatase, AR-LBD, ER-LBD, AhR) and five stress-response (ARE, ATAD5, HSE, MMP, p53) endpoints. The benchmark every toxicity ML paper compares against; our floor is mean AUROC ≥ 0.78 to match published RF/XGBoost baselines, ceiling ≥ 0.82 to beat them with deep models.

source: NIH NCATS via DeepChem · MoleculeNet
ToxCast
~ 9,000 compounds · 600+ assays

Broader in vitro panel from EPA's high-throughput toxicology program. We use the curated subset that overlaps with Tox21 (~3,500 compounds across 50 high-quality assays) for multi-task transfer learning — pre-train on ToxCast assay panel, fine-tune on Tox21 for the demo endpoints. Scaffold-holdout enforced.

source: EPA ToxCast / CompTox Dashboard
ClinTox
1,478 compounds · 2 endpoints

FDA-approved drugs vs. clinical-trial failures (CT_TOX) and clinical-toxicity events (FDA_APPROVED). Small but high-signal for "did this molecule survive humans" — a genuinely different question from the in-vitro endpoints in Tox21. Imbalanced (~6% positives); we use focal loss and report AUPRC alongside AUROC.

source: MoleculeNet · originally Gayvert et al. 2016
SIDER
1,427 marketed drugs · 27 system-organ classes

Adverse drug reaction labels mapped to MedDRA system-organ classes (cardiac, hepatobiliary, renal, etc.). ~ 13,000 compounds

DILIrank
~ 1,000 compounds · 4 severity classes

FDA's drug-induced liver injury classification: most-DILI-concern, less-DILI-concern, no-DILI-concern, ambiguous. Small dataset, so we use it strictly as test-set / external validation, never as primary training data.

PubChem (subset)
1,000,000 compounds · embedding only

Backbone of the molecular similarity index (CAP-04). We sample 1M compounds from PubChem Compound Database (filtered to drug-like by Lipinski Ro5 + MW between 100–800 Da + non-toxic metals excluded), embed with frozen MolFormer-XL, PCA-reduce to 128 dims, index in HNSWlib. No labels needed; this is a pure neighborhood store.

source: PubChem · NCBI · drug-like subset
PubMed (toxicology slice)
100,000 abstracts

Backbone of the biomedical RAG (CAP-02, CAP-03). Pulled via NCBI E-utilities filtered by the seven MeSH terms (Toxicity, DILI, hERG, Cardiotoxicity, Genotoxicity, Nephrotoxicity, Neurotoxicity). Chunked to 256-token windows with 32-token overlap, embedded with PubMedBERT, indexed in ChromaDB. Abstracts only; we never store full text (license discipline) but link out to DOI for full reads.

source: NCBI PubMed via E-utilities · MeSH-filtered
  • Provenance discipline: Each Parquet file under processed/features/v0/ in MinIO carries a sidecar .meta.json recording the source dataset version, the curation script git-hash, the SMILES count before and after each filter step, and the timestamp. This is what regulators ask for.
  • Conflict resolution: When the same canonical SMILES appears with disagreeing labels across datasets, we drop the compound entirely and log the rejection to audit_log with reason code conflict_drop. We never silently pick one label.
  • Scaffold overlap target: Tanimoto similarity between train and test scaffold sets must stay below 0.4 mean — verified on Day 4 as a precondition for any model training. If overlap exceeds 0.4 we re-split with a stricter scaffold algorithm.

§ 07 · governance & rituals

How we run the twenty days

Three engineers can communicate informally most of the time, but POCs go off the rails when nobody writes anything down. Below is the minimum ceremony we keep — nothing optional, nothing more.

We run a single Slack-style channel #poc-war-room for everything synchronous: standups, blockers, EOD checkpoints, ad-hoc help requests. A second channel #poc-stakeholders is broadcast-only — the team posts a Friday-EOD weekly summary and milestone announcements; stakeholders read but do not interrupt. All technical decisions of any consequence are written into docs/decisions/ as numbered ADRs (Architecture Decision Records, MADR template) so the next team can see why a choice was made, not just what it was.

The work week is Monday through Friday. Saturdays and Sundays are weekends and the schedule explicitly accounts for that — Day 1 is Thursday 7 May, Day 5 is Wednesday 13 May (skipping the 9–10 May weekend), and so on through Day 20 on Wednesday 3 June. Nothing reasonable should require weekend work; if something does, the standup the following Monday opens with why and what we change so it does not recur.

§ 08 · risk register

Top risks — named, owned, mitigated

Twelve risks identified up front. Each has a probability (P) and impact (I) score on a 1–5 scale; severity = P × I. Each has a single owner and a documented mitigation. The register is reviewed at every Wednesday mid-week sync; new risks join, mitigated risks retire.

We score impact in days-of-slip-equivalent: I=1 means < half a day, I=3 means a full day to two days, I=5 means a week or terminal-to-the-POC. We score probability against the twenty-day window, not against industry baselines. Severity ≥ 12 is a P1 risk and earns a daily check-in line in standup; severity 6–11 is P2 (Wednesday review only); below 6 is acknowledged-and-monitored.

Risks are specific. "Models might underperform" is not a risk; it is hand-waving. "GAT scaffold-holdout AUROC on Tox21 lands below 0.78, the published RF baseline" is a risk — we know what it means, who owns it, and what to do about it.

§ 09 · v1 roadmap

What we build after Day 20

The POC ends on Day 20 with a working, demo-able platform. V1 is the next horizon — six work-streams that take the architecture from "running on one host" to "a product a pharmaceutical company would buy and a regulator would audit." Each card below is sized in approximate engineer-weeks; the totals are deliberate — this is a quarter of work, not a sprint.

The POC architecture is intentionally V1-shaped. Every service is already containerized; the GPU node slots in via IOMMU passthrough on Day 21 morning; the OpenAPI contract is already typed and versioned; the audit log already records every prediction with a hashed input. Nothing in V1 requires us to throw away POC code — it is all addition, hardening, and scale.

The six horizons below are the real conversation we want to have on Day 20 with stakeholders, after the demo. We do not propose all six in the same breath; we propose the first two (GPU + generative design) as the obvious next quarter, the next two (multi-tenant + regulatory pack) as the quarter after that, and the final two (continuous learning + advanced explainability) as scope to negotiate based on customer pull.

Horizon 01 · 4–5 engineer-weeks

GPU acceleration & production model serving

Add an NVIDIA A100 (80 GB) or H100 node to the host (or replace the host with a GPU-equipped server). IOMMU-passthrough into a new vm-gpu-llm VM. Migrate vLLM (Llama-3.1-8B) and ChemBERTa fine-tunes onto it. Reclaim CPU on vm-rag-llm and vm-tox-inference for higher concurrency. Replace ad-hoc model loading with NVIDIA Triton Inference Server for batched requests, dynamic batching, and per-model auto-scaling.

Targets LLM throughput uplift 8–12× (from ~12 tok/s CPU to 120–150 tok/s GPU). ChemBERTa single-molecule inference from 250 ms to under 25 ms. Single-molecule end-to-end /analyze p95 latency from 12 s to under 3 s. Total downtime budget for the migration: 2 hours, executed at a stakeholder-agreed window.
Horizon 02 · 6–8 engineer-weeks

Generative design with toxicity-aware constraints

Add a generative front-end — REINVENT 4 (RNN-based reinforcement learning) and MolGPT (transformer language model on SELFIES) — that proposes new molecular candidates inside a chemical-space neighborhood the user specifies. The toxicity scorer from CAP-01 becomes the constraint: candidates with predicted hERG > 0.5 or DILI > 0.5 are pruned at sample time, not at filter time. Output is ranked by a multi-objective score (target activity + toxicity + synthesizability via SAScore).

Targets 1,000–5,000 valid candidates per design run, of which ≥ 60% pass toxicity gates and ≥ 40% are synthetically accessible (SAScore < 4). User flow: input target SMILES + objective (e.g., "improve hERG profile while keeping core pharmacophore"); output a ranked SDF with predicted properties and similarity to seed. Wires into the existing /analyze pipeline so every generated candidate gets a full toxicity report on the same UI.
Horizon 03 · 5–7 engineer-weeks

Multi-tenant SaaS with strict isolation

Convert the single-tenant POC into a multi-tenant deployment where each pharmaceutical customer gets its own logical workspace. Per-tenant Postgres schemas (one schema per tenant on a shared database, row-level security as a defense-in-depth layer); per-tenant MinIO bucket prefixes; per-tenant API keys with scoped JWT issuers; metering on every API call for billing. Tenant-isolated audit log so customer A never sees customer B's prediction history even by hash.

Targets Onboard a new tenant in under 15 minutes via a Terraform module. Per-tenant rate limits (100 req/min default, configurable per contract). Compliance attestations: SOC 2 Type 1 within 6 months, ISO 27001 within 12. Tenant deletion runs end-to-end in under 24 hours and produces a written attestation document for the departing customer.
Horizon 04 · 4–6 engineer-weeks

Regulatory pack — OECD QSAR · ICH M7 · REACH

Auto-generate the documentation a pharmaceutical regulatory submission actually requires. OECD QSAR Toolbox-aligned model documentation covering the five OECD principles: defined endpoint, unambiguous algorithm, defined applicability domain, statistics, mechanistic interpretation. Export to ICH M7 format for genotoxic-impurity assessments. Export to REACH dossier format for chemical-substance registrations. Every export includes the model card, the training data provenance, the calibration curves, and the per-prediction confidence intervals.

Targets Single-button export from the dashboard's compliance page. PDF + machine-readable XML side by side. Reviewed by an external regulatory consultant (named in V1 plan) before first customer use. Coverage of the top 12 toxicity endpoints regulators care about for small-molecule drug submissions.
Horizon 05 · 6–9 engineer-weeks

Continuous learning & data-feedback loop

Customers who use the platform produce signal: which predictions they agreed with, which they overrode, which compounds they synthesized and tested in the wet lab. We capture that signal (with consent) and use it to retrain the models on a quarterly cadence. Strict separation between training data and customer data: a customer can opt out of training contribution per workspace; even when contributing, the training pipeline never sees raw SMILES — it sees fingerprints + labels.

Targets Quarterly model refresh with a published changelog. Hold-out validation against a frozen reference set so customers can verify a refresh did not regress on their use case. Active-learning loop: low-confidence predictions get queued for human-in-the-loop review and become priority training examples. Drift monitoring on production embeddings to detect when the chemical space has shifted enough to require off-cycle retraining.
Horizon 06 · 5–7 engineer-weeks

Mechanistic explainability & AOP integration

Move beyond "this atom mattered" to "this molecule probably triggers this molecular initiating event, which propagates to this organ-level adverse outcome." Integrate the OECD Adverse Outcome Pathway framework: link every toxicity prediction to the most-probable AOP from the AOP-Wiki knowledge base. Wire in the ComptoxAI heterogeneous knowledge graph (compounds ↔ genes ↔ pathways ↔ assays) so the explanation panel can show "this compound is predicted toxic because it resembles known AhR agonists, which trigger CYP1A1 induction, which links to the hepatotoxicity AOP".

Targets Every prediction carries a top-3 AOP hypothesis with confidence scores and source citations. The dashboard's Explain page renders the AOP chain as a sankey diagram. Mechanistic explanations are validated against a 50-compound expert-curated benchmark set with target precision ≥ 75% (a known AOP appears in the top-3 for 75% of cases).

The roadmap conversation we are not having. We deliberately do not propose a clinical-trial-data integration, a wet-lab automation tie-in, or an FDA submission-as-a-service platform in V1. Those are V2+ scope and require regulatory partnerships and customer-funded development before we touch them. The POC and V1 are both squarely in the "in silico discovery and triage" lane — that is the lane where AI is currently delivering value, and the lane where we have evidence we can ship.

§ 10 · definition of done

The thirty-item checklist that gates Day 20 sign-off

A POC is done when these thirty items are checkable. Not before. Day 19 morning, the team walks this list together; whatever is not green is what Day 19 polishes. Day 20 morning the list is walked again with stakeholders observing; the green count is the deliverable measure.

Each item is binary — it is checkable or it is not. Aspirational language ("system is fast", "code is clean") is banned from this list; every item names a measurable outcome, an artifact in the repository or in MinIO, or a passing test. The list is written so that an outside auditor with read access to the repo and the running stack can verify every item without asking us a single clarifying question.

  • DoD-A2 CAP-02 Literature retrieval — POST /search/literature returns hybrid-ranked PubMed abstracts with MeSH-term filters. MRR@10 ≥ 0.55 on the 50-query gold set. p95 latency < 300 ms. verified by reports/retrieval-v0.md and tests/integration/test_search.py

Escalation ladder

Most issues never leave the engineer's desk. The ladder below describes how an issue rises if it cannot be resolved at the current rung. Time-on-rung is a soft target — the rule is "raise it before it becomes a slip", not "wait the prescribed time."

Rung 0 self
Engineer hits a problem. Tries the obvious things, searches the ChromaDB literature index for any prior solution in the codebase, checks the relevant ADR, reads the runbook. Spends up to 60 minutes here before raising. Most problems resolve on this rung; the discipline is to recognize when 60 minutes is up and not become heroic about it.
Rung 1 peer
Engineer pings the relevant lane partner in #poc-war-room with "stuck on X for 60 min, here's what I tried, can you eyeball?" Sarthak↔Harshit on model layer; Harshit↔Jitendra on services / infra interface; Jitendra↔Harshit on observability / data plane. The peer responds within 30 minutes during work hours; if they are deep in something else, they say so and propose a 14:00 huddle slot.
Rung 2 team
All three engineers in a 30-minute huddle. Triggered when a peer pair cannot resolve in 60 minutes, or when the issue crosses two lanes (e.g., a model produces NaN only when called via the API, not from a notebook — that is a Sarthak + Harshit problem with infra implications). Outcome: a fix, a workaround, or an explicit "this needs to escalate to stakeholders, here is the plan."
Rung 3 stakeholder
Issue is communicated to #poc-stakeholders. Reserved for: scope changes, schedule slips of more than one day, requests for stakeholder input or vendor budget. The post follows the no-surprises rule — problem stated, options listed, recommendation made, asks of stakeholders explicit. Stakeholders respond within 24 hours during work hours; if longer, the team picks the recommended option and proceeds with a "we are going ahead with X unless you tell us otherwise by Y" message.
Rung 4 terminal
The POC is in genuine danger. Reserved for: a security incident, irrecoverable data loss, an engineer becoming long-term unavailable, a fundamental capability failing to materialize (e.g., the LLM cannot produce coherent answers even on GPU). A direct call (not a Slack message) is placed by the lead engineer to the lead stakeholder. The conversation is followed by a written summary in #poc-stakeholders within an hour. The POC may be stopped, scope-cut hard, or extended; that is the stakeholder's call.
